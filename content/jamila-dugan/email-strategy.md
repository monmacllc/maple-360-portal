### Email 2: "The difference between stewarding and monetizing"
**Trigger:** Day 2

**Subject Line Options:**
1. Monetizing and stewarding are not the same thing
2. The word I stopped using — and what I say instead
3. What no one tells you about turning your expertise into a product

---

**Email Body:**

A few years ago, I sat with a colleague who had just been invited to create an online course.

She was brilliant — fifteen years of equity work embedded in her practice, a framework she'd been developing in community, a way of moving in rooms that was entirely her own. The invitation felt like recognition. It felt like arrival.

What followed was not what she expected.

The platform she chose had a template. The template had a format. The format had an expectation: deliver your content in digestible, actionable modules. Move quickly. Optimize for completion rates. The underlying logic was efficiency. The underlying frame was extraction — not of her, but by her. The assumption was that her knowledge was a product waiting to be packaged.

She packaged it. The course sold. And something was lost in the process that she couldn't fully name until years later.

I have thought about her often.

There is a difference between monetizing your expertise and stewarding it. Monetizing asks: how do I turn this into revenue? Stewarding asks: how do I hold this with integrity — while also building the infrastructure that lets it move in the world?

Stewardship does not preclude income. It precedes it. It says: before we decide how to share this, we need to know what this is, where it came from, who it belongs to, and what conditions will keep it alive when it travels without you.

Most frameworks for "building your course" skip all of that. They move directly to content organization, platform selection, and pricing. Those things matter. But they are second-order questions. The first-order question is about moral center.

I've been working on a framework that starts there. It's called the IP Stewardship Foundation, and it begins not with your offer — but with your inventory.

I'll share more in a few days.

In stewardship,
Dr. Jamila Dugan

### Email 4: "Why your frameworks keep getting diluted"
**Trigger:** Day 7

**Subject Line Options:**
1. Why your frameworks keep getting diluted — and what's actually happening
2. The thing that happens to your IP when it travels without you
3. On frameworks that get repackaged — and what to do about it

---

**Email Body:**

Let me describe something you may have already experienced.

You create a framework. You test it. You teach it — in workshops, in consulting engagements, in conversations with colleagues who carry equity work. It works. You watch it work. You can see, in real time, what it does in a room.

And then, somewhere along the way, it starts coming back to you in a form you don't recognize.

Someone references it without attribution. Someone else packages the idea under a different name. A colleague who attended your training starts teaching a version of it to their clients — altered, lightened, stripped of the relational and cultural context that made it what it was. You hear your own frameworks quoted at you by people who don't know where those ideas came from.

This is extraction. And it is not always malicious. Sometimes it is the product of a system that does not have adequate norms for attribution, for citation, for the acknowledgment of who produced what. But the outcome is the same: your IP becomes diluted, your labor becomes invisible, and the communities your work was designed to serve end up receiving a diminished version of something you built with care.

The answer is not to stop sharing. The answer is to steward.

Stewardship means building the infrastructure around your IP before it travels — naming it precisely, documenting it specifically, establishing the relational agreements that govern how it moves. It means knowing which parts of your framework are sacred (designed for specific communities and contexts) and which are trainable (can be delegated with full fidelity) and which are automatable (can be reproduced at scale without losing what matters).

Most practitioners never make these distinctions. They share everything — or nothing. Both are failures of stewardship.

I've built a framework for doing this well. More soon.

In stewardship,
Dr. Jamila Dugan

**Trigger:** Day 10

**Subject Line Options:**

2. Walking through two layers of your IP system
3. Inside the framework: two layers most people skip

---

**Email Body:**

Last week I introduced the problem of IP dilution — what happens when your frameworks travel without the infrastructure to protect them. Today I want to show you what the alternative looks like, using two of the seven layers as an example.

The 7-Layer IP Architecture Audit is the foundational assessment tool in the IP Stewardship Foundation. It maps the full scope of what you carry — across seven distinct categories of intellectual property that most practitioners have never examined as an integrated system.

Here are two layers most people undercount:

**Layer 2: Embodied Practice**

This is the knowledge that lives in your body — the facilitation moves, the room-reading skills, the intuitive sense of when to slow down and when to push. Most practitioners assume this layer is unteachable. It is not. But it requires a different kind of documentation than written frameworks. It requires naming the moves, creating deliberate practice protocols, and building the context that allows others to learn what you do — not just what you say.

When this layer is undocumented, it becomes the thing your clients can't replicate after you leave. It becomes the reason "they had something special" — and why your absence is catastrophic to the systems you've helped build.

**Layer 5: Lineage and Attribution**

This layer asks: where did your IP come from? Who taught you? Whose community wisdom lives in your frameworks? What was given to you that you have since built upon?

Most practitioners skip this layer entirely — not out of dishonesty, but because the intellectual property system we operate in treats knowledge as individual production. It is not. Your frameworks are a function of your teachers, your community, your lived experience, and the people who trusted you with their struggles. Documenting this layer is not about diminishing your contribution. It is about accuracy — and accuracy is the foundation of integrity.

Each layer you document adds to the infrastructure that allows your IP to move in the world without being extracted. Each one is an act of stewardship.

There are five more layers in the audit. I'll tell you about the full system soon.

In stewardship,
Dr. Jamila Dugan

### Email 6: "Sacred, Trainable, Automatable"
**Trigger:** Day 14

**Subject Line Options:**
1. Three categories that will change how you think about your IP
2. Not everything you know should be in a course
3. Sacred, Trainable, Automatable — a framework you can use today

---

**Email Body:**

There is a question I ask every practitioner before they build a course or launch a program or license their IP to an organization:

What, of everything you know, should not be productized?

That question usually produces a pause. People are not used to being asked to withhold. The dominant logic of the creator economy is total disclosure — share everything, package everything, sell everything. The goal is reach. The frame is abundance.

But stewardship asks a different set of questions. Not just "what can I share?" but "what should I share, with whom, under what conditions?"

The classification I use is this:

**Sacred.** This is IP that belongs to community — not to commerce. It emerged in relationship with specific people, in specific contexts, often in conditions of vulnerability. Packaging it for mass distribution would strip the meaning from the method. It is not for sale. It is held, honored, and passed down carefully.

This does not mean it is inaccessible. It means the conditions of access matter. Community first. Commerce, if at all, second.

**Trainable.** This is IP that can be systematically taught — to colleagues, to licensed practitioners, to students. It can be delegated with full fidelity if the learning is structured well. It can live in a course, a workshop, a training of trainers. The key word is "systematically": you can articulate how it works, why it works, and what conditions it requires.

This is the layer most courses are built from.

**Automatable.** This is IP that can be reproduced without losing essential meaning — templates, frameworks, tools, guides. It does not require your presence to deliver value. It scales. It is the layer that generates passive income.

Here is what I want you to do with this today: take the most prominent piece of IP in your practice and ask yourself — is this sacred, trainable, or automatable? Or is it, as is common, some combination of all three?

That distinction is where stewardship begins.

In stewardship,
Dr. Jamila Dugan

P.S. — This classification is one of the core frameworks in the IP Stewardship Foundation. The full course walks you through applying it to every layer of your IP system.

I want to tell you about something I've witnessed.

A practitioner — a consultant with more than a decade of equity work behind her, nationally recognized frameworks, a client list that included major school systems — came to the conclusion that her business model was not sustainable. Not because she lacked clients. Because everything she offered required her physical presence. Her income was entirely active. Her IP was entirely in her head.

When a district administrator she'd worked with for years left to start a consulting practice, he began offering training using language and methods that came directly from her work. She had nothing in writing. There was nothing to point to.

She did not blame him. She blamed the system she had built — a system without infrastructure.

We spent time working through the 7-Layer IP Architecture Audit together. Not to sue anyone. Not to close off access to her work. But to name what she had built, trace where it had come from, and establish the conditions under which it could travel.

That is not a small thing.

From there, she built a self-guided digital course that ran while she slept. She developed attribution agreements for the organizations she trained. She created a tiered access model — free for community practitioners, fee-based for institutions. She stopped giving her most sacred knowledge to the highest bidder and started determining her own conditions of access.

Her income did not triple overnight. Her relationships with clients deepened. Her sense of integrity about her work expanded. She began to build something that would outlast her.

That is what stewardship makes possible.

## Style Reference for All Emails

- Subject lines: Descriptive, not manipulative. No urgency language. No ALL CAPS. No emoji.
- Opening: Never with a thesis. Begin with story, image, question, or provocation.
- Tone: A thoughtful letter from a respected mentor. Unhurried.
- Length: 300-500 words. One idea per email. One CTA per email.
- CTAs: Gentle. "When you're ready." "More soon." Never "Don't miss this."
- Sign-off: "In stewardship, Dr. Jamila Dugan" — consistent across all emails.
- P.S.: Optional but effective when used for a secondary thought that does not fit the main body.
- Vocabulary: Follow writing style guide. No "monetize," "unlock," "limited time," "empower."
