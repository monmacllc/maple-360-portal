## Course Overview

"IP Stewardship Foundation" is a five-module digital course for educators, consultants, authors, and practitioners who have spent years producing knowledge — and who deserve a framework for holding that knowledge with intention. This course does not teach you how to hustle your expertise. It teaches you how to steward it.

Built around Dr. Jamila Dugan's three named frameworks — the 7-Layer IP Architecture Audit, the Stewardship Arrival protocol, and Moral Center Codification — this course is both theoretical and deeply practical. Students leave with a personal IP inventory, a values-aligned protection strategy, and a go-to-market approach grounded in integrity.

**Primary Audience:** Black women educators, consultants, and practitioners; POC knowledge workers; authors and national speakers who have not yet claimed or protected the full scope of what they carry.

## Module 1: Naming What You Carry

**Subtitle:** Foundations of IP Stewardship
**Theme:** Before you can protect or share what you know, you must be able to name it. This module builds the vocabulary, the framework, and the reflective foundation that the entire course rests on. It is slow on purpose. The work of naming is sacred.

**Estimated Module Runtime:** 15–18 minutes

### Lesson 1-1: Stewardship Arrival
**Brief Description:** A grounding meditation that prepares students to enter the work of naming what they carry — not as a task, but as an act of care.
**Duration:** ~45 seconds

**Learning Objectives:**
- Shift from production mode into reflective presence
- Establish the somatic and psychological conditions for deep work
- Name the module's theme (naming what you carry) before any content is introduced

---

### Lesson 1-2: What Is IP Stewardship?
**Brief Description:** A conceptual reframe — from "monetizing expertise" to "stewarding knowledge." Distinguishes stewardship from hoarding, extraction, and abandonment. Uses story first, then naming, then framework.
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes (300–400 words)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Define IP stewardship in contrast to extraction and abandonment
- Articulate why management and stewardship are fundamentally different postures
- Identify what is at stake when knowledge workers don't steward their IP
- Connect individual IP stewardship to collective liberation

---

### Lesson 1-3: The 7-Layer IP Architecture Audit
**Brief Description:** An introduction to Dr. Dugan's proprietary 7-Layer framework. Each layer is named and briefly described using cataloging and direct address. Students begin to orient their own IP relative to the layers.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes (400–500 words)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Name and briefly describe all 7 layers of the IP Architecture
- Recognize which layers they have historically over- or under-counted as IP
- Understand the architecture as an integrated system, not a checklist
- Begin to locate their own work within the framework

---

### Lesson 1-4: Knowledge Lineage
**Brief Description:** A reflective, emotionally resonant lesson on tracing the origins of your IP — ancestral knowledge, lived experience, community wisdom, formal training. Who taught you what you know? What was given, what was built, what was inherited?
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes (300–400 words)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Trace the sources and origins of their primary IP
- Distinguish between knowledge they built independently and knowledge held in community
- Name at least one "lineage debt" — knowledge given to them that shaped their work
- Begin practicing the acknowledgment protocols that stewardship requires

---

### Lesson 1-5: The Stewardship Inventory (First Pass)
**Brief Description:** A guided practical exercise: beginning to name and categorize what you carry using the 7-layer framework. This is a first pass — rough, incomplete, alive. Students are invited to sit with what they discover.
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes (300–400 words)

**Learning Objectives:**
- Apply the 7-Layer framework to their own IP in an initial, low-stakes inventory
- Identify at least 3 things they hadn't previously counted as IP
- Notice emotional responses that arise during the naming process
- Leave with a living document that will be built throughout the course

## Module 2: Moral Center Codification

**Subtitle:** Grounding Your IP in Values
**Theme:** Knowing what you carry is not enough. You must know what you stand for — and whether the ways you are sharing, licensing, and monetizing your IP align with that moral center. This module is where values meet practice.

**Estimated Module Runtime:** 16–20 minutes

### Lesson 2-1: Stewardship Arrival (Module 2)
**Brief Description:** The existing sample grounding meditation. Invites students into the module's values-centered work.
**Duration:** ~45 seconds

**Learning Objectives:**
- Re-enter focused presence before the values work begins
- Acknowledge the module's shift from naming to accountability
- Establish psychological safety for the values audit ahead

---

### Lesson 2-2: What Is a Moral Center?
**Brief Description:** Defines moral center not as a mission statement but as a living ethical core — the non-negotiables that determine what you will and will not do with your IP.
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Distinguish moral center from brand voice, mission statements, and marketing language
- Identify the difference between stated values and operational values
- Name 2–3 personal non-negotiables that currently govern their IP decisions
- Understand why moral center must be codified, not just internalized

---

### Lesson 2-3: The Values Audit
**Brief Description:** A structured exercise for auditing whether current IP decisions (who gets access, at what price, in what form) align with students' stated values.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Complete a four-quadrant values audit for their primary IP offering
- Identify at least one area of values misalignment currently present in their work
- Name the cost of that misalignment — to themselves and to their community
- Begin drafting a values alignment statement for their IP practice

---

### Lesson 2-4: Stewardship vs. Extraction
**Brief Description:** A frank examination of how extraction operates — including how well-intentioned knowledge workers can inadvertently participate in extractive systems. Uses Jamila's signature "story first" pattern.
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Define extraction in the context of knowledge work and IP
- Recognize extraction patterns that may exist in their current practice
- Distinguish between extraction and appropriate compensation
- Identify one move toward stewardship they can make immediately

---

### Lesson 2-5: Writing Your Moral Center Document
**Brief Description:** A guided writing exercise resulting in a 1–2 page Moral Center Document that students will reference throughout the rest of the course.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Draft a personal Moral Center Document using the provided template
- Articulate their non-negotiables, their permission zones, and their community commitments
- Connect their moral center to the IP inventory from Module 1
- Understand that this document is a living practice, not a one-time product

---

## Module 3: Architecture of Protection

**Subtitle:** Mapping, Documenting, and Holding Your IP
**Theme:** Protection is not paranoia. Protection is the infrastructure that allows your work to move in the world without being extracted. This module is practical and procedural — and it is also an act of love.

**Estimated Module Runtime:** 18–22 minutes

### Lesson 3-1: Stewardship Arrival (Module 3)
**Brief Description:** Grounding meditation that frames protection work as care, not fear.
**Duration:** ~45 seconds

**Learning Objectives:**
- Shift mindset from defensiveness to intentionality before the protection module
- Acknowledge that protection is an act of love, not a response to threat
- Prepare for the module's more procedural content with grounded presence

---

### Lesson 3-2: Mapping the Layers (Practical Exercise)
**Brief Description:** Students map their full IP across the 7 layers using a provided visual framework. This is the deep-dive companion to the first-pass inventory from Module 1.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Complete a full 7-Layer map for their primary area of IP
- Identify which layers are currently underdocumented or at risk
- Distinguish between what they hold individually and what is held in community
- Prioritize 2–3 layers for immediate protection focus

---

### Lesson 3-3: Documentation Systems That Work
**Brief Description:** Practical guidance on documentation — what to document, how often, in what format — without creating an overwhelming administrative burden.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Identify the minimum viable documentation system for their IP
- Understand the difference between internal documentation and legal documentation
- Set up a repeatable documentation rhythm that fits their actual capacity
- Know what to do when they create something new

---

### Lesson 3-4: Legal vs. Relational Protection
**Brief Description:** Honest conversation about what legal protections can and cannot do, and why relational protection — community agreements, attribution norms, explicit permissions — is equally important.
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand the scope and limits of legal IP protections (copyright, trademark, etc.)
- Name at least two forms of relational protection they can implement immediately
- Draft a basic attribution expectation for their primary IP asset
- Identify when to involve legal counsel vs. when relational agreements suffice

---

### Lesson 3-5: Drawing Boundaries With Integrity
**Brief Description:** How to say no — to partnerships, licensing requests, and collaborations that don't align with your moral center — without shame or over-explanation.
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Distinguish between a boundary and a barrier
- Articulate a boundary-setting framework grounded in values, not fear
- Draft language for one common IP boundary scenario
- Practice saying no as a stewardship act, not a defensive posture

---

## Module 4: Sacred, Trainable, Automatable

**Subtitle:** Deciding What to Share, What to License, What to Hold
**Theme:** Not everything you know should be in a course. Not everything should be licensed. Some of what you carry is sacred — it belongs only to the communities it was created with. This module teaches you to tell the difference.

**Estimated Module Runtime:** 17–20 minutes

### Lesson 4-1: Stewardship Arrival (Module 4)
**Brief Description:** Grounding meditation that invites students into the module's discernment framework.
**Duration:** ~45 seconds

**Learning Objectives:**
- Settle into the discernment work ahead
- Acknowledge the weight of deciding what to share and what to hold
- Arrive with both openness and intentionality

---

### Lesson 4-2: Introducing the Sacred–Trainable–Automatable Framework
**Brief Description:** An introduction to the classification system that governs sharing strategy. Defines each category with clarity and examples from education, consulting, and advocacy contexts.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Define Sacred, Trainable, and Automatable in the context of their own IP
- Understand why the distinction matters for stewardship, pricing, and access
- Identify how to apply the framework across different types of IP
- Recognize that classification can evolve over time

---

### Lesson 4-3: What to Share and How
**Brief Description:** A decision framework for sharing IP — in courses, talks, articles, social media — that centers community benefit and values alignment over reach metrics.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Apply the Sacred–Trainable–Automatable classification to their primary IP
- Develop a sharing strategy that reflects their moral center
- Identify which IP assets are ready for wider distribution and which need more protection
- Understand the difference between open access and abandonment

---

### Lesson 4-4: Licensing With Integrity
**Brief Description:** What licensing actually means, what a values-aligned licensing approach looks like, and how to build agreements that protect both the licensor and the communities they serve.
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Define IP licensing in plain language
- Understand what a values-aligned licensing clause looks like
- Identify at least one IP asset that may be appropriate to license
- Know what questions to ask before entering a licensing agreement

---

### Lesson 4-5: Community Agreements and Delegation Strategy
**Brief Description:** How to build community agreements around shared IP, and how to delegate knowledge sharing without losing the relational and cultural context that makes it powerful.
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Draft a basic community agreement for one piece of shared IP
- Understand when and how to delegate knowledge sharing
- Identify what context cannot be delegated and must travel with the IP
- Create a stewardship handoff protocol for collaborative work

---

## Module 5: Stewardship in Motion

**Subtitle:** Going to Market With Integrity
**Theme:** Everything you have built across four modules now meets the world. How do you share your work — publicly, commercially, in community — without losing what makes it yours? This module is both practical and philosophical. It closes the course with a return to why.

**Estimated Module Runtime:** 17–20 minutes

### Lesson 5-1: Stewardship Arrival (Module 5)
**Brief Description:** Final grounding meditation. Honors the work done. Prepares students to take what they've built into the world.
**Duration:** ~45 seconds

**Learning Objectives:**
- Honor the journey through the course before entering the final module
- Settle into a posture of possibility rather than urgency
- Prepare to hold their IP stewardship practice as an ongoing commitment

---

### Lesson 5-2: Pricing as Stewardship
**Brief Description:** A frank, values-grounded conversation about pricing — why underpricing is not humility, why overpricing is not confidence, and how to find a price that honors both your IP and your community.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Understand pricing as an expression of values, not just market positioning
- Identify the difference between accessibility and devaluation
- Apply a stewardship pricing framework to their primary offering
- Draft a pricing philosophy statement for their IP practice

---

### Lesson 5-3: Building Your Go-to-Market Plan
**Brief Description:** A practical, values-centered go-to-market framework — from identifying your right audience to choosing your channels to structuring your launch without urgency tactics.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Identify their primary audience and the stewardship relationship they want with them
- Choose 1–2 distribution channels aligned with their moral center
- Draft a 90-day go-to-market outline grounded in integrity, not hustle
- Distinguish between reach and resonance as success metrics

---

### Lesson 5-4: Building Community Around Your IP
**Brief Description:** How to build a community that deepens your IP rather than diluting it — and how to hold that community with the same stewardship ethic you've applied to your knowledge.
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Define what a stewardship community looks like vs. an audience
- Identify 2–3 practices for building community without extracting from it
- Understand how community feedback can evolve and deepen your IP over time
- Create one community onboarding norm grounded in your moral center

---

### Lesson 5-5: The Ongoing Stewardship Practice
**Brief Description:** The closing lesson. Returns to the opening question: what does it mean to steward what you carry? Closes the course with an invitation to an ongoing practice, not a finished product.
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes

**Learning Objectives:**
- Articulate their personal stewardship commitment going forward
- Identify a 90-day stewardship review practice they will maintain
- Connect their individual stewardship practice to collective liberation
- Leave with a sense of arrival — not urgency, not hustle, but earned possibility
