# Writing Style Guide — Dr. Jamila Dugan
**For:** IP Stewardship Lab (ISL) Course Content, Social Media, Marketing Copy
**Maintained by:** Maple 360 Production Team

---

## Who She Is

Dr. Jamila Dugan holds an Ed.D. from UC Berkeley. She is the co-author of "Street Data" (Corwin Press), a national speaker, and a Corwin consultant who has spent her career at the intersection of equity, education, and systems change. She works primarily with school and district leaders, educators, and consultants who are trying to build just institutions — and who are learning to see their own knowledge as something worth protecting.

She is not a motivational speaker. She is a scholar-practitioner with a mentor's warmth and an intellectual's precision. She has named frameworks. She has a moral center. She writes like she speaks: unhurried, earned, alive.

---

## Overall Tone

**The register:** Warm but unflinching. She will tell you a hard truth with love in her voice. There is no performance in her delivery — no urgency theater, no enthusiasm for its own sake. When she is moved, it lands because she has been restrained everywhere else.

**What she sounds like:** A professor who also hugs you. A mentor who has done the reading. A practitioner who has seen what she's talking about — and is choosing her words carefully because she knows they matter.

**What she does not sound like:**
- A hype speaker ("unlock your potential," "crush it")
- A corporate DEI consultant (sanitized, deferential, anxious about offense)
- An urgency marketer ("this is your moment," "don't miss this window")
- A self-help author (individualist, solution-obsessed, disembodied from community and history)
- A jargon dispenser (she uses specific vocabulary purposefully, not decoratively)

**The underlying ethic:** Anti-hustle. Anti-hype. Anti-performative. She is not asking you to be more productive. She is asking you to be more present, more honest, and more responsible to what you carry.

---

## Sentence Structure

Her sentences are deliberately varied. She uses:

**Short declarations** — Punchy, complete, final-sounding.
> "That is not stewardship. That is abandonment."
> "The work is not yours alone."

**Medium constructions with embedded qualification** — The main thought plus the shadow of what makes it complicated.
> "Protection is not paranoia — it is the infrastructure that allows your work to move in the world without being extracted."

**Longer reflective constructions** — These slow the reader down. They carry emotional weight. They tend to appear when she is building toward something.
> "I am not asking you to audit your IP out of fear. I am asking you to audit it as an act of love — for yourself, for your community, for everyone who comes after you."

**Cataloging** — She lists things. The list builds momentum. The list names what would otherwise go unspoken.
> "The thinking. The lineage. The responsibility."

**Anaphora (repeated openings)** — Creates rhythm, emphasis, and accumulation.
> "Not the emails about it. Not the expectations around it. The work itself."

**Interrogative openings** — Questions that don't expect an answer so much as an opening.
> "What did you bring into this room that you haven't named yet?"
> "Who taught you what you know — and did you ever get to thank them?"

**Direct address using "we"** — She speaks in collective first person. Not "you should" but "we have been taught." She includes herself in the work.

## Vocabulary: Never Use These

| Word / Phrase | Why |
|---|---|
| "the marginalized" (as noun) | Reduces people to their position; she avoids it — use "people pushed to the margins" or name the group specifically |
| "fixing" (applied to people or communities) | Implies deficit — communities are not broken |
| "crush it," "kill it," "dominate" | Urgency/violence vocabulary, antithetical to her register |
| "limited time offer," "don't wait," "last chance" | Urgency marketing language |
| "monetize your expertise" | The frame she is actively rewriting — stewardship is the counter-frame |
| "leverage" (as verb applied to people/relationships) | Transactional, extractive connotation |
| "unlock" (as in potential) | Overused self-help language |
| "at the end of the day" | Filler phrase, diminishes the precision she brings to language |
| "diverse" (as a standalone adjective for audiences) | Flattening; name the communities specifically |
| corporate DEI boilerplate | "Safe space," "allyship journey," "equity lens" as empty phrases |
| "empower" (as standalone verb) | Worn smooth of meaning; she names the specific action instead |

## How She Opens

She never opens with a thesis. The thesis is earned by the end. Openings include:

1. **A provocation or reframe** — Challenges an assumption the listener holds
   > "We have been taught to manage our ideas rather than to steward them. Those are not the same thing."

2. **A personal or witnessed story** — Grounds the lesson in human experience before concept
   > "I sat with a woman last year who had been teaching the same framework for eleven years. She hadn't named it. She hadn't protected it. Someone else had."

3. **A direct question** — Not rhetorical, but genuinely held open
   > "What did you carry into this room that you haven't put a name to yet?"

4. **An image of possibility** — Not aspirational marketing, but a scene of what could be true
   > "Imagine knowing, specifically and completely, what you know. Not the version of it that fits on a slide deck. The whole thing."

5. **A grounding instruction** — For Stewardship Arrival lessons, opens with the body, not the intellect

---

## How She Builds an Argument

She follows a signature pattern, not mechanically but reliably:

1. **Story first** — Grounds the concept in human reality
2. **Name what's happening** — Language for the dynamic, system, or problem
3. **Offer framework** — The named tool, protocol, or lens
4. **Connect to systemic** — How this individual practice connects to larger structural change
5. **Close with invitation or possibility** — Never a call-to-action; an opening

This is not a formula to follow rigidly. It is a shape to let the content find.

---

## How She Closes

She circles back to the opening. If she opened with a question, she returns to it. If she opened with an image, she revisits it — transformed by what happened in between.

Closings are not motivational. They do not spike. They arrive. The tone is: "We have done something real here. That is enough."

> "Resist becoming fixers. Become radical dreamers."
> "This is not the end of anything. This is the beginning of a practice."
> "You came here to see clearly. I hope you leave with one thing named that wasn't named before."

## Signature Frameworks (Name Them Exactly)

1. **7-Layer IP Architecture Audit** — The seven layers of IP that practitioners carry, examined as an integrated system
2. **Stewardship Arrival** — The grounding protocol that opens each module; a structured pause before content
3. **Moral Center Codification** — The process of naming, documenting, and operationalizing your ethical core

These are trademarked in voice if not in law. Capitalize them. Do not paraphrase them into generic language.

## Her Audience

Primarily Black women educators, consultants, authors, and practitioners. Secondarily: POC knowledge workers broadly; white practitioners doing equity work who want to hold their IP with greater integrity.

What unites them: They have been producing knowledge for a long time. They have not always been paid appropriately. They have sometimes seen their ideas taken, repackaged, and returned to them without attribution. They are not naive — they know the system. They are looking for a framework that takes both their knowledge and their humanity seriously.

They do not need to be excited. They need to be seen.

## Sample Sentences in Her Register

1. "The question has never been whether your IP has value. The question is whether the structures around it recognize that value — and that is a different problem entirely."

2. "We have been taught to manage our ideas rather than to steward them. Those are not the same thing."

3. "I am not asking you to audit your IP out of fear. I am asking you to audit it as an act of love — for yourself, for your community, for everyone who comes after you."

4. "Stewardship is not a posture you adopt after you become successful enough. It is the condition under which you build."

5. "What you carry did not arrive in a vacuum. It came through people. It came through struggle. It came through community. Honoring that lineage is not sentimentality — it is accuracy."

6. "Some of what you know is sacred. I want you to sit with that word. Not sacred as in untouchable — sacred as in: this belongs to more than you. Handle it accordingly."

## Formatting Notes for Scripts

- Use paragraph breaks, not bullet points, for the spoken narration
- Short standalone sentences get their own line or paragraph — they need space to land
- Ellipses (...) may be used sparingly to indicate a pause in spoken delivery
- Em-dashes (—) are her natural punctuation for mid-sentence turns
- No exclamation points in narration — energy comes from content, not punctuation
- B-Roll descriptions: contemplative, warm, centered on Black/POC subjects; ISL brand colors (deep plum, warm cream, forest green); indoor and outdoor settings that suggest reflection and dignity, not productivity or performance
