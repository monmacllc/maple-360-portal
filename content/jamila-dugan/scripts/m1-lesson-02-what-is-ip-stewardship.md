# M1-L02: What Is IP Stewardship?

**Title:** What Is IP Stewardship?
**Module:** Module 1 — Naming What You Carry
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes (~350 words)

---

## Narration Script

I want to tell you about a conversation I had with a woman — a veteran educator, thirty years in the field, two named frameworks, a book in process — who told me, without any irony, that she didn't think she had any intellectual property worth protecting.

She had been giving it away. Freely, gratefully, believing that generosity meant having no walls.

I understood her completely. Because we are taught — especially those of us who build knowledge for communities rather than markets — that holding onto what we know is selfish. That the point is to share. That protecting your IP is something corporations do, not educators. Not practitioners. Not people whose work is about liberation.

But here is what I have come to believe: stewardship and generosity are not opposites. What is opposite to stewardship is not sharing. What is opposite to stewardship is abandonment.

Let me name the three postures we often fall into without realizing it.

Extraction: we give our knowledge to systems and institutions that take more than they offer back. We train rooms full of people in frameworks we built — sometimes without being named, sometimes without being paid, almost always without our permission being asked.

Hoarding: we hold our knowledge so tightly, out of fear or exhaustion or distrust, that it stops moving. It doesn't reach the communities it was meant to serve. Protection becomes isolation.

Abandonment: we produce and produce and produce without ever stopping to see what we've built. We don't name it. We don't document it. We don't hold it as something that belongs to us and to the people who gave it to us. We let it drift.

Stewardship is the fourth thing. Stewardship means knowing what you carry, knowing where it came from, knowing what it is for — and making intentional decisions about how it moves in the world. Not fearful decisions. Not hoarding decisions. Intentional ones.

We have been taught to manage our ideas rather than to steward them. Those are not the same thing.

Management is reactive. Stewardship is relational. Management asks: how do I protect this? Stewardship asks: who does this belong to, and how do I honor that?

That is the question this course is built to help you answer.

## B-Roll Scene Descriptions

1. A Black woman educator at the front of a large room, speaking to a crowd of peers. The audience is engaged, leaning in. The image conveys the scene of someone sharing deeply held knowledge freely — beautiful and also slightly exposing.

2. An open hand releasing a small paper bird into the air outdoors. The gesture is not dramatic — it is quiet, deliberate. Warm afternoon light. The image holds the ambiguity between release and loss.

3. A library shelf seen from below, looking upward. Books densely packed, spines showing varied colors and titles. The shot implies accumulated knowledge, legacy, weight. One hand reaching toward the shelf, not quite touching.

4. A woman sitting across a large table from a person in institutional clothing (suit, clipboard). Her posture is attentive but slightly guarded. The framing suggests a negotiation — knowledge worker meeting institution.

5. A Black woman's hands holding a spiral notebook open to a page of handwritten notes. The notes are dense, a life's work visible in miniature. She is reviewing them with care. The image suggests: this matters. This was built. This is hers.
