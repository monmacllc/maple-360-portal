# M1-L03: The 7-Layer IP Architecture Audit

**Title:** The 7-Layer IP Architecture Audit
**Module:** Module 1 — Naming What You Carry
**Duration:** ~3–4 minutes (~450 words)

---

## Narration Script

Here is something I have noticed: when I ask knowledge workers what their intellectual property is, they almost always start in the middle.

They name the course. The book. The keynote. The framework that has a name and a slide deck.

They start in the middle because those are the things that have already been recognized — by publishers, by event organizers, by institutions willing to cut a check. Those are the things the market has already affirmed.

But what you carry is not only what the market has already named.

The 7-Layer IP Architecture Audit is a framework I developed to help practitioners see the full scope of what they carry — not just the visible surface, but the whole structure underneath it. Seven layers. Each one real. Each one yours. Each one deserving of stewardship.

Let me name them.

**Layer 1: Lived Experience.** The things that happened to you — and through you — that no one else can replicate. Your body of experience is not anecdote. It is primary source material. It is IP.

**Layer 2: Ancestral and Community Knowledge.** What was given to you by lineage — by family, by elders, by community, by culture. This layer is sacred. It carries obligation as well as ownership.

**Layer 3: Formal Training.** Your degrees, certifications, clinical training, research expertise. The credential is the container, but the deep knowing is what lives inside it.

**Layer 4: Named Frameworks.** The ones with titles. The ones you can point to. The auditable, teachable, reproducible systems you have built. This is where most people start the inventory. It is Layer 4, not Layer 1.

**Layer 5: Methodologies and Processes.** How you do the thing, not just what the thing is. The sequence. The facilitation moves. The way you enter a room, read a system, hold space for a community. This is harder to name — which is exactly why it needs to be named.

**Layer 6: Teaching and Learning Materials.** Every curriculum, workshop deck, rubric, assessment tool, and coaching protocol you have ever built. Some of it is documented. Some of it lives in your muscle memory. All of it is IP.

**Layer 7: Embodied Wisdom.** The knowing that doesn't fit in a document. The thing you know because of what you've survived, carried, witnessed. It is real, it is distinct, and it is yours — even when the world has not yet built the container to hold it.

Seven layers. Not a hierarchy — a whole. The work of this course is to help you see all seven clearly, so you can hold all seven with intention.

Some of you are already stewarding Layer 4 with great care and leaving Layers 1, 2, and 7 completely unnamed.

That changes now.

## B-Roll Scene Descriptions

1. An overhead shot of seven small objects arranged on a wooden surface — a feather, a worn textbook, a small framed photo, a folded paper framework diagram, a handwritten process map, a curriculum binder, and a smooth stone. Each object represents a layer. Warm, intimate lighting. The arrangement is deliberate, not decorative.

2. A Black elder speaking quietly to a younger woman. Both are seated at a kitchen table. The elder's hands are moving gently — the gesture of passing something down. The feeling is: lineage in motion.

3. A close-up of a facilitator's hands during a workshop — they are mid-gesture, directing, inviting, holding space. The hands are expressive. The body knows what to do. This is embodied wisdom made visible.

4. A woman seated before a large whiteboard covered in a process map she has drawn. She is pointing to a specific section, studying it. The map is complex and beautiful. The image conveys: this was built. This took years.

5. A double-exposure or layered image (if achievable in generation): a woman's face overlaid with shelves of books and handwritten notes, suggesting the depth and breadth of what one person can carry. Warm tones. The feeling is abundance, not burden.
