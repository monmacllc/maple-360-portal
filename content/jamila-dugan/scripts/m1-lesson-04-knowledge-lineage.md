# M1-L04: Knowledge Lineage

**Title:** Knowledge Lineage
**Module:** Module 1 — Naming What You Carry
**Duration:** ~2–3 minutes (~340 words)

---

## Narration Script

There is something I want us to hold before we go any further.

None of what we carry arrived in a vacuum.

Not the frameworks we've named. Not the research we've conducted. Not the ways we enter a room or read a system or hold a community through a hard thing. All of it came through people. Through struggle. Through time. Through bodies and kitchens and classrooms and churches and places that never once made it into our academic citations.

I want to name that.

I think about the teachers who sat with me in ways that no one was paying them to sit. The community members who trusted me with their street data — their real stories, their real grief, their real insight — long before I had a framework to hold it. The elders who handed me something without being asked, because they could see that I was ready to carry it.

My IP is not only mine. It never was.

And I do not think that is a diminishment. I think that is the most honest and generative thing I can say about my work — that it was shaped by more people than I can name, and that the best thing I can do with it is carry it carefully enough that the people who come after me receive something worth carrying.

This is what we mean by knowledge lineage. Not a genealogy project. A reckoning. An accounting. A practice of asking: where did this come from? Who held this before I did? What do I owe — not as debt, but as gratitude and responsibility?

We are not the originators of most of what we know. We are the current stewards of it.

And stewardship, at its root, is about answering the question: what will be true about this knowledge because I held it?

What will be better? What will be more protected? What will have reached further, more carefully, more honestly?

That is the question knowledge lineage asks us to live.

And that is a worthy thing to spend a career answering.

## B-Roll Scene Descriptions

1. A Black woman seated on a porch, an elder woman next to her. They are not performing — they are simply present together, mid-conversation. The elder is gesturing; the younger woman is listening with her whole body. The image is of transmission — something being given from one generation to the next.

2. A worn, heavily annotated book open on a lap. The margins are full of handwriting — two different hands visible, different ink colors, different generations of notes. The book is a living record of how knowledge passes through people.

3. A community gathering space: folding chairs in a circle, a few people in conversation. The room is intimate, not formal. On the walls, handmade signs and photographs of community members. The image conveys: this is where knowledge lives before it has a name.

4. A Black woman standing before a large family photograph — formal, multi-generational, spanning decades. She is looking at it carefully, not nostalgically. Her posture suggests: I am tracing something here. I am reading this as a source.

5. Two hands — one older, one younger — passing a folded piece of paper between them. The exchange is gentle, deliberate. The paper could be anything: a letter, a document, a map, a framework. The image is of stewardship as an act of care between people.
