### Post 1: The Reframe
**Theme:** Rejecting "monetization" as the frame for sharing expertise
**Best time to post:** Tuesday or Wednesday, 8–10am

---

I refuse to accept the framing that our intellectual property needs to be "monetized."

Not because income doesn't matter. It does.

But because the word "monetize" carries a logic inside it — a logic that says your knowledge is a raw material waiting to be processed into product. That the move from practitioner to course creator is primarily a transaction. That what you know is most valuable as a revenue stream.

I've spent fifteen years watching what happens to practitioners who accept that framing. Their frameworks get simplified to fit a template. Their relational knowledge gets flattened into bullets. The context that makes their methods work in rooms gets stripped out to improve completion rates.

What's left may still sell. But it is not what they built.

Stewardship is a different posture. It asks: what conditions would allow your IP to move in the world without losing what makes it alive? What infrastructure does it need — not just to generate revenue, but to remain faithful to the communities it was built with?

Those are not the same questions. They do not produce the same outcomes.

I am not anti-income. I am anti-extraction. There is a way to build a sustainable, values-aligned knowledge practice that generates real revenue while protecting what matters. That is what I'm building infrastructure for.

What word do you use — or wish you could use — instead of "monetize"?

### Post 2: The Distinction
**Theme:** Protecting vs. hoarding IP — the ethical difference
**Best time to post:** Monday or Thursday, 9–11am

---

The difference between protecting your IP and hoarding it is the same difference between building a home and building a fortress.

One is designed for habitation — for life, for others, for movement in and out. The other is designed for defense.

I have met practitioners who have gone so far into protection mode that they cannot share their work with the communities it was created for. They are so afraid of extraction that they have become extractive themselves — hoarding knowledge that could be in circulation, keeping frameworks locked away that could be in use.

That is not stewardship. That is fear.

Stewardship asks a harder question: how do I hold this with enough integrity that it can move freely — and still remain mine?

The answer is infrastructure. Documentation. Clear attribution expectations. A classification of what is sacred, what is trainable, and what is automatable. A moral center that governs every decision about access and sharing.

When that infrastructure exists, you can be generous. You can share widely. You can license with confidence. You can teach your methods to others and trust that what you've built is protected not because it's locked away, but because it's fully known — by you.

Protection and generosity are not opposites. They are partners.

What would it feel like to share your IP as freely as it deserves — because you trust the infrastructure around it?

### Post 3: Bridge from Street Data
**Theme:** What "Street Data" taught about the value of qualitative knowledge — applied to IP
**Best time to post:** Any weekday, 8–10am — especially when posting alongside book content**

---

Street Data taught me that what is measurable is not the same as what is valuable.

The book was built on that argument: that schools had become so focused on standardized metrics that they had stopped seeing the qualitative, humanizing data that lives in stories, in relationships, in the margins of what gets officially counted. We argued that the data closest to the student — the "street data" — was the most important data of all.

The same is true for your intellectual property.

The IP that gets counted — the published book, the named framework, the keynote you've given forty times — is the surface layer. It is real. It is significant. But it is not the whole of what you carry.

Beneath the measurable IP is a vast, largely undocumented body of knowledge. The facilitation moves you've refined over fifteen years. The room-reading skills that no methodology captures. The relational knowledge — how you build trust quickly in high-stakes rooms, how you hold conflict with dignity, how you know when to slow down and when to push.

None of that is on a resume. None of it is in a copyright filing. All of it is yours.

Naming what you carry — the full scope of it, across all seven layers — is the first act of stewardship. It is also, I believe, an act of radical honesty about the true value of what you've produced.

### Post 8: Kingston Story
**Theme:** Personal — what a child taught about stewardship
**Best time to post:** Sunday or Monday — warm, personal tone works well at week boundaries**

---

My son Kingston taught me something about stewardship that no Ed.D. program ever could.

He was young enough that the lesson came without commentary. We were in the kitchen, and he was showing me something he had made — a drawing, a structure, something built with intention — and he stopped me before I could take a closer look.

"Wait," he said. "Let me tell you what it is first."

He wanted me to see it on his terms. Not through my interpretations, not through my frame. Through his explanation first, his naming first. He had made something and he needed to be the one to introduce it.

That is stewardship.

That is what we forget to do with our own intellectual property. We share it before we have named it. We let others interpret it before we have described it. We release it into the world and then watch it come back to us in forms we don't recognize — because we never took the moment to say: let me tell you what this is first.

The act of naming is not about control. It is about fidelity — to what you built, to what you intended, to the community you built it with.

Kingston is older now. He still shows me things that way. And every time, I think about what it would mean if we all demanded the same dignity for our work: the right to introduce it ourselves, on our own terms, before anyone else gets to decide what it means.

What would it look like to reintroduce your most important IP — on your own terms?

### Post 12: Sacred vs. Trainable vs. Automatable
**Theme:** The classification framework — educational and practical
**Best time to post:** Tuesday or Thursday**

---

Sacred, Trainable, Automatable.

These are three categories that change how you think about sharing your IP — and which of your frameworks should live in a course, which should be licensed, and which should never be commodified at all.

**Sacred** is the knowledge that belongs to community. It emerged in specific contexts, with specific people, often in conditions of vulnerability or trust. It cannot be packaged for mass distribution without losing what makes it meaningful. It is not for sale. It is held, honored, and passed on carefully — if at all.

When you encounter your Sacred IP, your job is not to monetize it. Your job is to protect it from those who would.

**Trainable** is knowledge that can be systematically taught — with full fidelity — to others. You can articulate how it works, why it works, and what conditions it requires. It can live in a course, a workshop, a certification program. It can be delegated to licensed practitioners.

This is the layer most courses are built from. But knowing what is and is not trainable prevents you from packaging something sacred as if it were trainable — a mistake that dilutes the knowledge and breaches the trust of the communities it came from.

**Automatable** is knowledge that can be reproduced without losing essential meaning. Tools, templates, guides, frameworks. It scales. It does not require your presence. It is the layer that generates passive income — and the layer most appropriate for open or wide distribution.

The practice is applying this classification to every major piece of IP you hold — and then building your sharing strategy from that honest inventory.

What in your current practice might be Sacred that you've been treating as Trainable?

### Post 13: 3 Signs of Extraction
**Theme:** Helping practitioners recognize when extraction is happening to them
**Best time to post:** Wednesday or Friday — practical diagnostic posts work well**

---

3 signs your IP is being extracted — not stewarded.

Not all extraction is deliberate. Some of it is systemic — the product of an intellectual culture that treats knowledge as ambient, as air that anyone can breathe without accounting for where it came from.

But the effect on practitioners — particularly Black women and practitioners of color who have been producing equity knowledge for decades — is the same regardless of intent.

Here are three patterns to watch for:

**1. Your language is in circulation, but your name is not.**

You hear your frameworks, your terminology, your pedagogical moves cited in conversations or proposals or trainings — without attribution. The ideas are there. You are not. This is a documentation and attribution failure — and it is yours to address, not just theirs.

**2. Organizations you've trained are now offering your methods.**

You delivered a training. The training was good. The team internalized it. And now, a year later, they are facilitating your methods with their own clients — without a licensing agreement, without attribution, without any recognition that the source of that methodology is you.

**3. You have given more to an engagement than you were paid for.**

You agreed to deliver a workshop. What you actually delivered was also your lineage research, your years of community relationship, your embodied facilitation knowledge, your unreplicable way of reading rooms. You were paid for two hours. The value you delivered was not capturable in two hours.

That gap is extraction.

The antidote in every case is infrastructure — naming, documentation, community agreements, licensing terms, and a clear moral center that governs what you give and under what conditions.

Which of these have you experienced?

### Post 15: 30-Minute Stewardship Audit
**Theme:** Practical, immediate value — how to do a quick self-audit
**Best time to post:** Thursday or Friday — practical content at week end**

---

How to do a stewardship audit in 30 minutes.

This is not the full 7-Layer IP Architecture Audit — that takes longer and goes deeper. This is a first pass. A way of beginning to see what you carry.

Set a timer for 30 minutes. Work through these five questions:

**1. What do I know that I've never written down?**

Not the polished frameworks. The embodied stuff. The facilitation moves, the room-reading skills, the ways you navigate specific kinds of conflict or resistance. List them. Don't explain them yet — just name them.

**2. What have I taught in the last twelve months that no one could have taught the same way?**

Not just the content — the way. The sequencing, the pacing, the specific moves you made in specific moments. What was irreplaceable about your delivery?

**3. What did I build inside an organization that I believe is mine?**

This is the question most people avoid, because the answer is complicated. But ask it. Note it. Understanding what you produced inside a system is the beginning of understanding what you carry out of it.

**4. What knowledge do I hold that came through community — and what are my obligations to that community as I share it?**

This is the lineage question. Not as guilt — as accuracy. Knowing where your IP came from tells you something about the conditions under which it should move forward.

**5. What has been taken from me that I never fully named as mine?**

The uncredited frameworks. The unreferenced ideas. The methods that traveled without you. Name them now. They are still yours.

Thirty minutes. Five questions. The beginning of a stewardship practice.
