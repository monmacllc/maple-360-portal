## Order of Presentation

This matters. Do not deviate from this sequence.

**1. Video first** — no words. Just play the video.

Before you say anything about the project, play the Stewardship Arrival video (Module 2, Lesson 1). Let her watch it. Say: "I want to show you something before we talk about anything else."

The video is 45 seconds. Let the silence after it hold. Ask: "What was that like to see?"

Her reaction to the video determines the tone of the rest of the meeting. If she's moved, stay with that. If she has questions about production quality, answer them directly. If she has a revision, note it and move on — it doesn't change the plan, it just informs the execution.

**2. Pitch deck / landing page** — walk her through `/landing/isl`
- This is the live page her audience would see
- Walk from top to bottom: headline, lead magnet offer, offer architecture, revenue projections, milestone plan
- Let her read, don't read it to her
- Ask: "Does this feel like you? Does this feel like the work?"

**3. Milestone plan** — milestone-plan.md (or the version at the bottom of the landing page)
- Walk through the 4 milestones at a high level
- Emphasize: "You can stop at any point. Everything we build is yours to keep."
- Emphasize: "We don't go to M2 until you've seen M1 working."
- Point to the proof gates explicitly — she is risk-averse and proof gates directly address that

**4. Q&A** — open floor, no agenda

**5. Close** — soft ask
- "Does this feel like the right direction?"
- "If you wanted to start, what would be your timing?"
- Never: "Are you ready to sign?" Never urgency. Never pressure.

## Talking Points — What to Emphasize

**"You have zero direct competitors."**
We analyzed 21 programs. Nobody occupies the intersection of IP stewardship + ethical framing + justice-oriented practitioners. This is not a crowded space that's hard to break into. This is an open field.

**"The timing is unusual."**
28,696 nonprofit jobs cut in 2025 — 4x the 2024 rate. These are equity professionals being pushed out of institutions who need exactly what this course offers. They're not coming back to institutions. They're going independent. They need IP stewardship education now, not in three years.

**"Your IP is already created."**
She doesn't have to write a course from scratch. The frameworks are named. The article is written. The audit PDF is designed. Module 1 is scripted. We're building the delivery system and the production pipeline, not the content.

**"The passive income math is conservative."**
$4,950/month requires 10 course sales/month. For someone with her national speaking platform and credibility, that is not an aggressive number. The projection assumes no Corwin Press cross-promotion, no paid advertising, no podcast appearances — only LinkedIn organic and her existing email list.

**"You control the pace."**
Maximum 2 cohorts per year. Maximum 5 intensive clients. We've built the model around her stated capacity. Nothing in this plan requires her to be more available than she is.

**"Every milestone is a proof gate."**
She does not have to trust us with $7,000 on Day 1. She commits to M1 ($2,000), sees it working, then decides about M2. This is milestone-based by design — it was designed for someone who is appropriately skeptical.

## Anticipated Questions and How to Answer Them

---

**"Why $495 for the self-guided course? That feels high."**

It's actually on the lower end for a 25-lesson course with this level of academic and professional credibility behind it. The reference point isn't "how much does an online course cost?" — it's "what would it cost to get this level of guidance elsewhere?" A single hour of her consulting time is more than $495. A seat in her cohort is $2,500.

Also: the price sends a signal. Underpricing signals low confidence in the value. We priced it at $495 because her IP architecture warrants it, and because pricing is itself a stewardship act — that's actually the subject of Lesson 5-2.

---

**"What if the course doesn't sell?"**

A few things to say here. First, we're not starting with 100 cold strangers — we're starting with her existing network (speaking contacts, Corwin relationships, JoySource members, LinkedIn connections). These are warm leads who already respect her work.

Second, the welcome email sequence and LinkedIn content are designed to build the list before the course is available. We're not launching to zero. By the time the course is live (end of M2), she'll have had 5 weeks of email list building.

Third, the risk is structured: M1 is $2,000. If nothing happens in M1 — no email signups, no article engagement, no interest — she has the option to stop before M2. She doesn't owe us $7,000 if M1 doesn't perform.

---

**"What about my relationship with Corwin? Will this compete with 'Street Data'?"**

No. This course is about IP stewardship — a meta-skill. It doesn't compete with Street Data's content. If anything, it positions her as the scholar who practices what she preaches: someone who has written about equity and systemic change and is now applying that same rigor to her own knowledge economy.

The Corwin relationship is potential upside, not a constraint. If Corwin ever promotes this to their author community as a resource for professional development, that's a distribution channel we haven't priced in.

---

**"Who owns the IP we produce together?"**

She does. All content, all videos, all course materials, all email sequences — everything Maple 360 produces for ISL belongs to Dr. Jamila Dugan. We retain no rights to her content. Maple 360's value is ongoing production and marketing support, not IP ownership.

We should have a simple service agreement in writing before M1 starts. This is standard and protects both parties.

---

**"How much of my time does this actually take?"**

Across the full 7-week build (M1 through M3): approximately 15–18 hours. That's roughly 2–2.5 hours per week, mostly in 15–30 minute review chunks, not long work sessions.

During M4 (ongoing retainer): approximately 30–45 minutes per month. We do the production, she reviews and approves.

The course runs without her once it's built. That's the point.

---

4 new LinkedIn posts per week (16/month), written, designed, scheduled. 2 short-form videos per month. Email sequence monitoring and optimization. Monthly analytics and revenue report. Platform maintenance. Content repurposing (blog, audiograms, quote graphics). Quarterly 60-minute strategy call.

For comparison: a single LinkedIn ghostwriter alone charges $1,500–$3,000/month. This includes video production, email optimization, analytics, and strategy.

The sample video is the answer to this question. Play it before addressing it verbally.

Beyond the sample: the voice clone (Shayla) is calibrated to match the warmth and pacing of her register — slow, warm, unhurried. It is not a robotic text-to-speech voice. It is a carefully tuned voice production.

If after seeing the sample video she still has concerns, we can offer to record a small set of lessons in her own voice (she reads the script, we handle everything else). That is a production variant we can accommodate, though it would add to her time commitment.

**"What about Kajabi's fees? Is this going to cost me more than I think?"**

Kajabi pricing: $119–$199/month (depending on plan). For the volume of students projected in Year 1, the mid-tier plan is sufficient. That cost is hers to carry — it's her course platform, her account, her transaction records.

Maple 360's costs are the milestone fees plus the retainer. There are no hidden production costs — the video pipeline costs are less than $3.25 for the entire 25-lesson course (ElevenLabs TTS + Gemini image gen).

**"What if I want to move slower? Do I have to follow the 7-week timeline?"**

No. The timeline is a target, not a contract. Milestones can be stretched if she needs more time between proof gates, if she's traveling for speaking engagements, or if she wants more revision cycles. The price doesn't change. The plan adjusts to her life.

The only cost of moving slower is the timing: 28,696 displaced DEI professionals represent a 12–18 month window of urgency. The sooner the course is live, the larger the market she captures.

This one may not be asked directly, but it may be felt. Address it proactively.

Everything produced is contractually hers. Nothing goes anywhere without her approval. The production pipeline is air-gapped to ISL — we don't share her content, her scripts, her strategies, or her client list with anyone. ## What to Avoid Saying

**Never say:**
- "You need to move fast" or any urgency framing
- "This is a limited opportunity"
- "Once the DEI window closes, you'll miss it" (the underlying timing argument is valid; urgency framing of it is not)
- "We can crush it on LinkedIn"
- "Let's monetize your expertise"
- "Unlock your earning potential"
- "This is your moment"
- "You'd be leaving money on the table"

**Be careful with:**
- Revenue projections — present them as conservative estimates based on industry data, not guarantees
- Comparison to competitors — stay factual; the competitive analysis is strong and doesn't need hype
- Kajabi or Stripe setup as a dealbreaker — if she doesn't have these, it's fine, she can get them. Don't make it sound like infrastructure is the only thing between her and success.

## Tone Check — How to Read the Room

Dr. Dugan is a scholar-practitioner. She has spent her career in rooms where people perform confidence they don't have, make promises they can't keep, and use urgency to close deals they're not sure about. She will feel inauthenticity immediately.

The posture for this meeting:
- **Confident but not aggressive.** We have done good work. We can speak to it plainly.
- **Curious, not closing.** Ask questions. Listen to what she cares about. Don't steamroll toward the contract.
- **Transparent about the unknowns.** If she asks whether the course will definitely sell — don't oversell. Acknowledge the honest answer: the data suggests strong demand, but nothing is guaranteed, which is why the proof-gate structure exists.
- **Stewardship-aware.** You are pitching a stewardship product. Act accordingly. Don't hustle her into a course about not being hustled.

The energy for the close: "We have done something real here. If it resonates, we'd love to build it with you. Take whatever time you need."

If she wants to think about it, give her the week. Don't follow up in 24 hours with pressure. Give her 5–7 days, then a light check-in: "I wanted to circle back and see if you had any questions after our conversation."
